The History of Cookie Clicker

Origins

Founder

Julien Thiennot, better known by his online alias Orteil, released Cookie Clicker in August 2013. It began as a simple browser experiment inspired by incremental (idle) games—titles where progress continues with minimal player input.

Initial Release

The game was initially hosted on Orteil's personal website and quickly gained traction due to its addictive gameplay loop. Players clicked on a giant cookie to produce more cookies, which could then be spent on upgrades and buildings to automate cookie production.

Viral Explosion

Within weeks, Cookie Clicker spread rapidly across forums like Reddit and 4chan. Its success came from a few key design choices:

  • Simplicity: The game was easy to understand and play, making it accessible to a wide audience.
  • Progression: The constant upgrades and new buildings provided a sense of accomplishment.
  • Humor: The quirky writing and whimsical upgrades added charm to the experience.

Feature Expansion & Depth (2014–2016)

As its player base grew, Orteil expanded the game far beyond a joke

Major Additions Included:
  • Buildings (Grandmas, Farms, Factories, Temples, Antimatter Condensers)
  • Golden Cookies (random events introducing risk/reward mechanics)
  • Achievements (meta-goals encouraging obsessive optimization)
  • Grandmapocalypse – a surreal, Lovecraftian phase where the game becomes hostile, self-aware, and morally unsettling

The Grandmapocalypse marked a turning point. Cookie Clicker stopped being “just numbers” and became satire—a commentary on unchecked growth, exploitation, and capitalism itself.

Steam Release & Continued Support (2018–Present)

In 2021, Cookie Clicker launched on Steam, featuring:

  • Enhanced graphics and sound design
  • Cloud saves for cross-device play
  • New content, including buildings, upgrades, and seasonal events

The Steam version sold hundreds of thousands of copies, demonstrating that even a decade-old browser game could thrive commercially.
